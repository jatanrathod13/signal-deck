## Detailed Analysis

### Dependency Accuracy (Weight: 0.35)

**Evidence Found:**

1. **Step 1 and Step 2 Contradiction**: Step 1 says "Parallel with: Step 2" and "Depends on: None". Step 2 says "Parallel with: Step 1" and "Depends on: None". Yet Step 2 also says "Dependencies: Step 1" (line 719). This is a direct contradiction -- it cannot both be parallel with Step 1 AND depend on Step 1.

2. **Step 3 dependency on Step 1 is questionable**: Step 3 (TypeScript Interfaces) says "Depends on: Step 1" (test infrastructure). The stated blocker is "Step 1 (test infrastructure needed for type validation)". But defining TypeScript interfaces does not require test infrastructure -- `npx tsc --noEmit` works independently. This is a false dependency.

3. **Step 8 (AgentService) inconsistency**: "Depends on: Step 7, Step 3" yet "Parallel with: Step 7, Step 9, Step 10". It cannot both depend on Step 7 AND be parallel with Step 7. The diagram shows Step 8 below Step 7 (sequential), which contradicts "Parallel with: Step 7".

4. **Step 17 (useSocket) inconsistency**: "Depends on: Step 16, Step 7" yet "Parallel with: Step 7, Step 8, Step 9, Step 10". Cannot depend on Step 7 and be parallel with it simultaneously.

5. **Step 18 (API Client) depends only on Step 3 (types)**: This is correctly identified as a loose dependency. However, the diagram shows Step 18 after Step 17 in the visual flow, which is misleading.

6. **Step 7 (SocketService) says "Parallel with: Step 8, Step 9, Step 10, Step 17"**: But Step 8 depends on Step 7. Steps 9 and 10 do NOT depend on Step 7 (they depend on Steps 4 and 3). So Steps 9 and 10 are genuinely parallel with Step 7, but Step 8 is NOT parallel with Step 7 -- it depends on it.

7. **Step 14 (BullMQ Worker) depends on Step 9 and Step 7**: This is correct -- it needs the queue service and socket service.

8. **Step 15 (React App) depends only on Step 5**: Correct. But the diagram places it far down the chain, after Step 14, and says "Parallel with: Step 14". The "Parallel with" is correct since they have different dependency chains.

**Analysis:**
There are at least 4 cases of steps listed as both "Depends on: X" and "Parallel with: X" for the same step X, which is logically impossible. Step 3's dependency on Step 1 is a false dependency. The diagram shows some correct relationships but contradicts the step-level annotations in several places. These are not minor issues -- they would cause confusion during execution.

**Score:** 3/5

The plan gets the broad strokes right (routes depend on services, services depend on config/types, UI depends on hooks), but the specific annotations contain multiple logical contradictions that would confuse executing agents.

**Improvement Suggestion:**
Remove all cases where a step is listed as both "Depends on: X" and "Parallel with: X". Step 3 should depend on Step 2 (needs package.json for tsc), not Step 1. Fix Step 8 to clearly depend on Step 7 (not parallel). Fix Step 17 similarly.

Start Steps 3, 4, 5, 16, and 18 all in parallel after Step 2 completes (they all only truly need types or package.json). This would increase the parallelization depth at the foundational layer.

### Agent Selection Correctness (Weight: 0.20)

**Evidence Found:**

1. **Available agents**: The context specifies: `sdd:developer`, `sdd:code-explorer`, `sdd:researcher`, `sdd:qa-engineer`, plus general agents `opus`, `sonnet`, `haiku`.

2. **Agents used in the plan**: Only `haiku` (Steps 1, 2) and `opus` (all other steps). No specialized agents used.

3. **Step 1 (Test Infrastructure) uses haiku**: This step creates package.json with Jest dependencies, jest.config.js, test setup files, and placeholder tests. While not deeply complex, configuring Jest with TypeScript presets and module path mappings requires some judgment. Haiku may be borderline appropriate.

4. **Step 2 (Project Config) uses haiku**: Creating package.json files with specific dependency lists. This is more mechanical -- haiku is reasonable.

5. **Steps 25 and 26 (Testing/Verification)**: These are quality assurance steps. The available `sdd:qa-engineer` agent would be the ideal choice, but `opus` is used instead.

6. **No use of `sdd:developer`**: Steps 6-14, 16-22 all produce source code. `sdd:developer` is explicitly available for source code implementation. Every one of these steps should use `sdd:developer` instead of generic `opus`.

7. **No use of `sdd:code-explorer`**: Could be useful for Step 24 (Connect Frontend to Backend) which involves inspecting and wiring existing code.

8. **No use of `sonnet`**: Steps 11, 12, 13 (REST routes) are similar in pattern and could use `sonnet` for high-volume, similar work. The three route files follow the same Express router pattern.

9. **The execution directive at lines 484-489 references "haiku, sonnet, opus"** as agent types but never mentions the specialized agents from the allowed list.

**Analysis:**
The plan completely ignores all four specialized agents (`sdd:developer`, `sdd:code-explorer`, `sdd:researcher`, `sdd:qa-engineer`) that were explicitly provided as available. Using only `opus` and `haiku` is a significant miss. The specialized agents exist precisely for these use cases -- developer for code, qa-engineer for testing/verification. This represents a fundamental failure to follow the provided agent list.

**Score:** 2/5

Only general agents used despite 4 specialized agents being available. This is below acceptable -- the rubric specifically asks "Are only agents from the provided available agents list used?" and while technically opus/haiku are allowed, the intent is clearly to use the specialized agents where appropriate.

**Improvement Suggestion:**
Assign `sdd:developer` to all source code implementation steps (6-14, 16-22). Assign `sdd:qa-engineer` to Steps 25 and 26. Consider `sonnet` for the three parallel route steps (11, 12, 13) which are repetitive patterns. Consider `sdd:code-explorer` for Step 24 (integration wiring).
